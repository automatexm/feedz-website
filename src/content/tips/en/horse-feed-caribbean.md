Horses on Sint Maarten face a different set of challenges than their counterparts in Europe or North America. The combination of year-round heat, high humidity, and limited access to quality pasture means your feed choices matter more here than almost anywhere else.

## The Caribbean Challenge

In a temperate climate, a horse might get 60–70% of its nutritional needs from pasture grazing. On Sint Maarten, that same horse might get 20–30% at best — and the grass that is available tends to be lower in nutrients than the lush meadow grass you'd find in the Netherlands or the UK.

This means your concentrate feed has to do more work. But piling on calories isn't the answer — overweight horses in hot climates are at higher risk for laminitis, heat stress, and joint problems.

## What to Look For

**Digestible energy, not just raw calories**

Look for feeds with highly digestible fiber sources — beet pulp, alfalfa, soy hulls — rather than feeds heavy in starch and sugar. In the Caribbean heat, horses process fiber more efficiently than starch, and it generates less metabolic heat during digestion.

**Electrolyte balance**

A horse sweating in 32°C heat loses significant sodium, potassium, and chloride. A feed with a good electrolyte profile — or a separate electrolyte supplement — is not optional here. It's essential.

**Protein quality**

Horses in work need good quality protein for muscle maintenance. Look for lysine and methionine on the label — the amino acids that actually matter, not just a high crude protein percentage.

**Vitamin E and selenium**

These two work together as antioxidants. Horses in hotter climates tend to need more antioxidant support. Check that your feed includes adequate levels, or supplement separately.

## Practical Tips

- **Feed smaller, more frequent meals.** Two larger meals is more practical, but if you can do three smaller ones, your horse's digestive system will thank you.
- **Always have fresh water available.** Horses drink 30–50% more in tropical heat. A horse that isn't drinking is a horse that isn't eating well either.
- **Store feed in a cool, dry place.** Mold grows fast in Sint Maarten's humidity. A sealed bin in a shaded, ventilated space protects your investment.
- **Watch your horse's condition score.** Not the weight per se, but the condition. Ribs slightly felt but not visible = good. Adjust feed accordingly.
