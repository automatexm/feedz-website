Backyard chickens are everywhere on Sint Maarten. They're low-maintenance, they give you eggs, and they're a practical part of island life. But "low-maintenance" doesn't mean "no maintenance" — and feed is where most people get it wrong.

## Layer Hens vs. Meat Birds

The first question is what you're keeping them for.

**Layer hens** (hens kept for eggs) need a feed with around 16–18% protein and — critically — adequate calcium. Calcium is what makes eggshells. Without enough of it, your hens either stop laying or produce soft-shelled eggs that break inside them. Layer pellets are formulated with this in mind; starter chick feed is not.

**Meat birds** (broilers) grow fast and need a higher protein feed — 20–22% — to support muscle development. They also need more energy per kilogram of feed. Broiler starter and finisher formulas handle this automatically.

**Dual-purpose birds** — the mixed local chickens most people keep — do fine on layer feed once they're past 18 weeks old.

## The Calcium Trap

One of the most common mistakes we see: people switch young hens to layer feed too early (before 18 weeks) or keep older hens on grower feed too long.

- **Before 18 weeks:** Feed starter or grower. Too much calcium too early damages developing kidneys.
- **After 18 weeks or first egg:** Switch to layer feed. The calcium demand is now active.

If your hens are laying thin or soft shells despite being on layer feed, supplement with crushed oyster shell in a separate dish. They'll self-regulate.

## Heat Stress in the Tropics

Sint Maarten's heat hits poultry harder than you might expect. Chickens don't sweat — they pant. When temperatures climb above 30°C:

- They eat less and drink more
- Egg production drops
- Eggshell quality decreases

To manage this:
- **Feed in the cooler parts of the day** — early morning and late afternoon
- **Always keep fresh water available** — they'll drink 2–4x more in heat
- **Avoid high-energy feeds during peak heat** — the metabolic heat of digestion adds to their heat load
