Most people notice their dog's coat changes before anything else does. Before weight loss, before low energy, before a vet visit — the coat signals. If you know what to look for, you can catch nutritional issues early and often fix them with a simple feed change.

## The Healthy Coat Baseline

A well-nourished dog has a coat that is:
- **Shiny** — not dull or flat
- **Smooth or characteristically textured** — consistent with the breed
- **Firm to the touch** — not brittle or breaking
- **Minimally shedding** — some is normal, constant heavy loss is not
- **Skin underneath:** pale pink and supple, no flakes, no redness

This is what good nutrition looks like from the outside.

## What Goes Wrong and Why

**Dull, dry coat with flaky skin**

The most common complaint we hear. Usually points to a deficiency in omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids. These essential fats — particularly from fish oil (omega-3) and chicken fat or linoleic acid (omega-6) — are what makes a coat gleam.

If you're feeding a cheap dry food with no named fat sources, this is likely your problem. Look for foods that list specific fats: "salmon oil," "chicken fat," "flaxseed." "Animal fat" as the only listed fat is a red flag.

**Excessive shedding**

Some shedding is seasonal and normal. But if your dog is losing fur year-round at a rate that seems excessive, look at:

1. **Protein quality and quantity** — coats are made of keratin, which is protein. Low-quality or low-quantity protein means the body deprioritizes coat growth.
2. **Zinc** — deficiency causes hair loss and poor skin condition.
3. **Overall caloric intake** — dogs that are mildly undernourished shed more.

**Brittle, breaking fur**

Fur that snaps rather than bends usually signals protein deficiency specifically. Check the protein percentage on your food (dogs need 18–22% minimum for maintenance, more if active or pregnant), and check the protein sources — egg and meat are more bioavailable than plant protein.

**Redness, itching, or patchy loss**

These can be dietary but also indicate allergies — either food or environmental. Common food allergens in dogs: beef, dairy, chicken, wheat, eggs. If a feed change doesn't resolve it in 8 weeks, see a vet.

## Making the Change

If your dog's coat isn't where it should be, here's a practical approach:

1. **Switch to a higher-quality food** with named protein and fat sources. Give it 8 weeks — coat cycles take time.
2. **Add a fish oil supplement.** Half a teaspoon of salmon oil daily for a medium dog. You'll often see results in 3–4 weeks.
3. **Check caloric needs.** Active dogs need more food than the bag suggests.
